These days, I have been doing a small project in terms of sub-Riemannian limits on contact manifolds. I came to learn that there was a profound relationship between contact structures and open book decompositions on 3-manifolds (see [1]). It leads to some topological applications of contact geometry. While reading about the open book decomposition, I came across a result due to Alexander. Its formal statement is

Theorem 1 (Alexander [2]). Every closed oriented 3-manifold admits an open book decomposition.

Before going through its proof, let me briefly revisit the concept of an open book decomposition. From now on, I always assume that $latex M$ is a closed, oriented 3-manifold.

Definition 1. An open book decomposition (OBD) of $latex M$ is a pair $latex (B,\pi)$ satisfying two following conditions.

  • $latex B$ is an oriented link in $latex M$ called the binding of the open book.

  • A fibration $latex \pi: M \setminus B \rightarrow S^1$ such that each fiber $latex \pi^{-1}(\theta)$ is the interior of a compact surface $latex \Sigma_{\theta} \subset M$, which is called a page, and $latex \partial \Sigma_{\theta}=B$ for all $latex \theta \in S^1$.

Let's look at one of the most fundamental examples of an OBD on $latex S^3$. Assume $latex S^3$ is embedded in $latex \mathbb C^2$, and $latex (z_1, z_2)= (r_1 e^{I\theta_1}, r_2 e^{ i\theta_2})$ are coordinates on $latex \mathbb C^2$. Let $latex U$ be an unknot in $latex S^3$ defined by $latex U:= \{z_1 =0\} = \{r_1=0\} \subset S^3$. The fibration $latex \pi_U: S^3 \setminus U \rightarrow S^1: (z_1,z_2) \mapsto \frac{z_1}{|z_1|}$ gives an OBD for $latex S^3$.

There is a nice way to visualize this OBD. Since $latex S^3$ is the compatification of $latex \mathbb R^3$, the unknot $latex U$ can be viewed as a straight line in $latex \mathbb R^3$. We attach $latex U$ to half-planes along their boundaries and through the compactification, these half-planes transform into the pages of the OBD.

Returning to Theorem 1, its proof relies heavily on two following facts about 3-manifolds.

Proposition 1 (Alexander [2]). Every closed, oriented manifold is the branched cover of $latex S^3$ over some link $latex L \subset S^3$.

Proposition 2 (Alexander [2]). Every link in $latex S^3$ can be braided about the unknot.

In terms of the second fact, a link $latex L \subset S^3$ is braided about the unknot means that $latex L$ can be isotoped so that $latex L \subset S^1 \times D^2=S^3 \setminus U$ and is transverse to pages $latex \{p\} \times D^2$ for all $latex p \in S^1$.

Let's call the cover in Proposition 1 by $latex P: M \rightarrow S^3$. First, define the binding $latex B: = P^{-1}(U)$. Since $latex P$ lifts pages of $latex S^3$ to pages of $latex M$, the map $latex \pi =\pi_U \circ P: M \setminus B \rightarrow S^1$ provides the fibering of $latex M \setminus B$.

The proof seems to be very elegant and straightforward, yet it still leaves me pondering about the actual role of the link $latex L$ in the proof. Does it play any role beyond being the branch locus of the covering $latex P$? And why is the transversality condition of $latex L$ necessary?

In the remainder of this post, I am not going into details of the proof but instead attempting to interpret the necessity of the transversality condition for the link $latex L$. I am very grateful for the insightful discussion with Professor Nathan Dunfield on this topic.

Assume that $latex L$ fails to be transverse to every page in $latex S^3$. Then there exists a page, say $latex P_1$ where one component of $latex L$ is tangent to $latex P_1$. In this case, there might be pages $latex P_0$ and $latex P_2$ just below and above $latex P_1$, respectively, with different homotopy types, as the number of intersection points with $latex L$ of $latex P_0$​ compared to $latex P_2$​ increases by 2 (see the figure below). Away from the branch locus, the lifting of fibers, namely $latex P_0$ and $latex P_2$, are not homotopy equivalent. Therefore, $latex \pi$ fails to be a fibration.

Another reason might come from the tangency of $latex L$ and $latex P_0$ itself. We can think of what happens when lifting into $latex M$ as a double cover of $latex S^3$. Upstairs, the preimage of $latex P_1$ is no longer a surface as it would be singular at the preimage of the tangency of $latex L$ and $latex P_0$.

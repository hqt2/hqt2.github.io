The Atiyah-Singer index theorem is one of the greatest mathematical findings of the 20th century. It was proven in 1963 by Sir Michael Atiyah and Isadore Singer, and played an important role in Atiyah being awarded the prestigious Fields Medal three years later. Many sophisticated results across various fields can be derived immediately from the A-S Theorem, such as the Riemann-Roch theorem in Algebraic Geometry, the Chern-Gauss-Bonnet theorem, the Hizebruch signature theorem in Differential Geometry. This series of posts summarises what I have learned about the A-S Theorem throughout this year. Since I am not an expert in this field, mistakes are, of course, unavoidable, yet hopefully, what I am conveying can be beneficial to readers.

Symbols and Elliptic operators

Let's start by reviewing briefly about Elliptic operators. The simplest example of an Elliptic operator is the Laplacian. Heuristically, the Laplacian serves as a toy model when studying something related to Elliptic Operators. So let's consider the Laplacian on one-dimensional Euclidean space $latex \mathbb R$

$latex \Delta = \dfrac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}.$

We have that the Fourier transform of $latex \Delta u$

$latex \displaystyle \mathcal{F}(\Delta u)(\xi) = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{\mathbb R} \Delta u(x) e^{-i\xi x} dx = (i\xi)^2 \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{\mathbb R} u(x) e^{-i\xi x} dx = -|\xi|^2 \mathcal{F}(u). $

The factor $latex -|\xi|^2$ is called the total symbol of the Laplacian $latex \Delta$. By applying the inverse Fourier transform on both sides, we obtain that

$latex \Delta u(x) = \dfrac{1}{2\pi} \iint_{\mathbb R \times \mathbb R} e^{i(x-y)\xi} (-|\xi|^2) u(y)dy d\xi. $ (1)

Generally, for an arbitrary differential operator of order $latex k$ on $latex \mathbb R^n , P = \sum_ {|\alpha| \leq k} a_{\alpha} (x) D^{\alpha},$ it can be proved that

$latex \displaystyle Pu(x) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}( \sigma(P)(x,\xi) \mathcal{F}(Pu))(x) = \dfrac{1}{(2\pi)^{n}} \iint e^{i(x-y) \cdot \xi} \sigma(P)(x,\xi) u(y) dy d\xi, \forall u \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb R^n) , $ (2)

where the total symbol $latex \sigma(P)(x,\xi) = \sum_{|\alpha| \leq k} a_{\alpha}(x) i^{|\alpha|} \xi^{\alpha}$ is simply a polynomial in $latex \xi$ with coefficients depending on $latex x$ obtained by replacing the differentiation $latex D^{\alpha}$ by $latex (i \xi)^{\alpha}$ . For convenience, we always assume the differential operators acting on Schwarz functions. The leading term of $latex \sigma(P)(x,\xi)$, $latex \sum_{|\alpha|=k} a_{\alpha}(x) \xi^{\alpha}$ denoted by $latex \sigma_{k} (P)$, is called the principal symbol of $latex P$. An important property of the principal symbol map is that it is a group homomorphism of differential operators on $latex \mathbb R^n$. That is if $latex D_1,D_2$ are differential operators of orders $latex \ell_1, \ell_2$ respectively, then

$latex \sigma_{\ell_1+ \ell_2}(D_2 \circ D_1) = \sigma_{\ell_2}(D_2) \cdot \sigma_{\ell_1}(D_1). $ (3)

Although the Laplacian is not invertible, its principal symbol is if $latex \xi \neq 0$. In general, we call a differential operator of order $latex k, P$ elliptic if its principal symbol $latex \sigma_k(P)(x,\xi)$ is invertible for every $latex \xi \neq 0$. A natural question to ask is whether there is an operator such that its symbol is the inverse of Laplacian's. It seems odd, but by (3), if it exists, this operator must be of order -2. It motivates us to define a class of differential operators in a broader sense.

Oscillatory integral

As in the expression (2), by an abuse of notation, the action of the differential operator $latex P$ can be viewed as the action of the oscillatory integral $latex \int e^{i(x-y)\cdot \xi} \sigma(P)(x,\xi) d\xi$ by mean of distribution although this integral might not be absolutely convergent. With this correlation, we can define operators called pseudodifferential operators in terms of oscillatory integrals. The key point is to start with what we want to end up with. Let $latex U \subset \mathbb R^n$ be an open set and $latex r \in \mathbb R$ a real number. A function $latex u \in C^{\infty}(U \times \mathbb R^n)$ is a symbol of order $latex r$, denoted by $latex a \in S^r(U ; \mathbb R^n)$, if for all multi-indices $latex \alpha, \beta$ there is a constant $latex C_{\alpha, \beta}$ such that

$latex \displaystyle \sup_{U} |D_{x}^{\alpha} D_{\xi}^{\beta} a(x, \xi)| \leq C_{\alpha, \beta} (\sqrt{1+ |\xi|^2})^{r - |\beta|}.$

Given a symbol $latex a \in S^r(U;\mathbb R^n)$, we want to make sense of the oscillatory integral

$latex \int e^{i(x-y)\cdot \xi} a(x, \xi) d\xi$

as a distribution.

The following basic fact is helpful. Recall that the integral $latex \displaystyle \int_{\mathbb R} (\sqrt{1+|\xi|^2})^{\ell} d\xi < \infty$ iff $latex \ell < -n$. The action of the oscillatory integral is

$latex \displaystyle \phi \in C^{\infty}_{c}(U) \mapsto \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \dfrac{1}{(2\pi)^n} \int e^{i(x-y)\cdot \xi} \psi(\epsilon \xi) a(x,\xi) \phi(x) d\xi dx,$ (4)

where $latex \psi \in C^{\infty}_{c}(\mathbb R^n)$ is a cut-off function which is equal to 1 in a neighbourhood of the origin. It is easy to check this is well-defined by integration by parts and the following observation

$latex (1+ \xi \cdot D_x) e^{i(x-y)\cdot \xi} = (1+|\xi|^2) e^{i(x-y)\cdot \xi}.$

A pseudodifferential operator $latex \mathcal{K}$ of order $latex r$ is defined as some oscillatory integral

$latex \mathcal{K} = \dfrac{1}{(2\pi)^n} \int e^{i(x-y)\cdot \xi} a(x,\xi) d\xi$

for $latex a$ is a symbol of order $latex r$.
